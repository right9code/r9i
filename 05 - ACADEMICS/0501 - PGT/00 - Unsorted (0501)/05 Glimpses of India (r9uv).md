## Part I: A Baker from Goa (Lucio Rodrigues)

### 1. Character Characteristics
*   **The Pader (The Baker)**: 
    *   **Historical Legacy**: He is the living link to the Portuguese era. While the "eaters of loaves" (the Portuguese) have left, the "makers" (the bakers) remain.
    *   **Visual Identity**: Traditionally wore the **Kabai** (a single-piece long frock). Later, he wore a shirt and trousers shorter than full-length—a style so iconic that anyone wearing similar trousers today is called a *Pader*.
    *   **Socio-Economic Status**: Baking was a **profitable profession**. The baker’s "plump physique" (described as **jackfruit-like**) was a public sign of his prosperity. 
    *   **Community Role**: He acted as a "friend, companion, and guide" to the children.

### 2. Detailed Plot Chronology
*   **Phase 1: The Morning Jingle**: The baker arrived twice a day. His morning entry was marked by the **"thud and jingle"** of his specially made bamboo staff. 
*   **Phase 2: The Social Interaction**: He supported the basket on his head with one hand and banged the bamboo with the other. He would greet the lady of the house with a "Good morning" and deliver the loaves to the servants (**Paskine or Bastine**).
*   **Phase 3: The Childhood Scramble**: Children would run to him, not for the bread, but for the **bread-bangles (bolinhas)** which were sometimes made of sweet bread.
*   **Phase 4: The Cultural Necessity**: The narrative details how bread was mandatory for life events:
    *   **Marriages**: Required the sweet bread called **bol**.
    *   **Engagements**: The mother had to prepare **sandwiches**.
    *   **Christmas/Festivals**: **Cakes and bolinhas** were essential.
*   **Phase 5: The Financial Routine**: The baker maintained accounts on **walls with pencil**. He collected his total bills at the end of the month.

### 3. Exam-Style Value Points
*   **The "Unextinguished Fire"**: The furnaces still exist, proving that essential traditions survive political changes.
*   **The Bread-Culture Relationship**: Bread in Goa is not just food; it is a social requirement for religious and familial ceremonies.

## Part II: Coorg (Lokesh Abrol)

### 1. Character Characteristics
*   **The Kodavus (Coorgis)**: 
    *   **Martial Heritage**: A "proud race of martial men" and beautiful women. They are the only people in India permitted to **carry firearms without a license**.
    *   **Greek/Arabic Lineage**: Their origin is traced to Alexander’s army (Greek) or the Arabs, evidenced by the **Kuppia** (long black coat with an embroidered waist belt) which resembles the **Kuffia** worn by Arabs and Kurds.
    *   **General Cariappa**: The first Chief of the Indian Army was a Coorgi, highlighting their military excellence.
*   **The Monks**: Inhabitants of the Tibetan settlement at **Bylakuppe**, wearing red, ochre, and yellow robes.

### 2. Detailed Plot Chronology
*   **Phase 1: The Geography**: Coorg (Kodagu) is the smallest district of Karnataka, located between **Mysore and Mangalore**. It is described as a **"piece of heaven"** that drifted from the kingdom of God.
*   **Phase 2: The Climate & Nature**: It is famous for **evergreen rainforests** (covering 30% of the land), spices, and coffee. The best time to visit is from **September to March**.
*   **Phase 3: Wildlife & Adventure**: The river **Kaveri** flows through the region, home to the **Mahaseer** (large freshwater fish). The land offers high-energy sports: river rafting, canoeing, rappelling, and mountain biking.
*   **Phase 4: The Panoramic Ascent**: A walk up the **Brahmagiri hills** offers a misty view of the entire Coorg landscape. 
*   **Phase 5: The Island of Nisargadhama**: A **sixty-four-acre island** reached across a rope bridge, leading to the Tibetan settlement and the quest for the "heart and soul of India."

### 3. Exam-Style Value Points
*   **The Hospitality Tradition**: Coorgis have a tradition of recounting tales of valor related to their sons and fathers.
*   **Cultural Hybridity**: The Kuppia/Kuffia connection is a crucial factual point for short-answer questions regarding their ancestry.

## Part III: Tea from Assam (Arup Kumar Datta)

### 1. Character Characteristics
*   **Rajvir**: A student from Delhi who is a "keen fan" of detective stories but is more interested in the **botanical history and legends** of tea.
*   **Pranjol**: A local Assamese boy whose father manages the **Dhekiabari Tea Estate**. He is less excited by the scenery because he has grown up in it.
*   **Pranjol’s Father**: An organized manager who is impressed by Rajvir’s knowledge of the "second-flush."

### 2. Detailed Plot Chronology
*   **Phase 1: The Rail Journey**: The boys travel to **Mariani Junction**. Rajvir notes that over **80 crore cups** of tea are drunk daily worldwide.
*   **Phase 2: The Visual Transition**: The scenery changes from green paddy fields to a **"sea of tea bushes."** Rajvir sees an "ugly building" (a tea factory) with smoke billowing out.
*   **Phase 3: The Historical Legends**:
    *   **The Chinese Legend**: An emperor (2700 B.C.) who boiled water had tea leaves fall in by accident.
    *   **The Indian Legend**: **Bodhidharma** (a Buddhist ascetic) cut off his eyelids to stay awake during meditation; ten tea plants grew from them.
*   **Phase 4: The Global Spread**: Rajvir explains that tea came to Europe in the **16th century**, initially used as a **medicine** rather than a beverage.
*   **Phase 5: The Harvesting Season**: They arrive during the **second-flush or sprouting period** (**May to July**). Rajvir correctly identifies this as the period that yields the best tea.

### 3. Exam-Style Value Points
*   **Second-Flush Significance**: The May-July window is the most profitable and high-quality period for tea production.
*   **Knowledge as Preparation**: Rajvir’s character highlights that "doing one's homework" enhances the experience of travel.

### I. Glimpses of India Class 10 Question Answer: A Baker from Goa: Oral Comprehension Check (Page 86)

**Question 1: What are the elders in Goa nostalgic about?**

**Answer:** In Goa, the older generation cherishes fond memories of the past when Portuguese influence was strong, particularly their appreciation for the famous bread loaves. Despite the disappearance of those who used to enjoy these loaves, the tradition of making them still persists among bread makers in the region.

**Question 2: Is bread-making still popular in Goa? How do you know?**

**Answer:** Bread-making continues to be popular in Goa, as evidenced by the presence of bread makers who have upheld this tradition. Despite the absence of the once-avid consumers of bread loaves, the artisans responsible for mixing, moulding, and baking the loaves, as well as the enduring furnaces used in the process, serve as a testament to the continued existence of this cherished practice.

**Question 3: What is the baker called?**

**Answer:** A baker is popularly known as a 'pader' in Goa.



**Question 4: When would the baker come every day? Why did the children run to meet him?**

**Answer:** Every day, the baker would make two rounds in the neighbourhood. In the morning, he would set out with a full basket of bread, and later, he would return after selling all his loaves. Excited children eagerly awaited his arrival, running to meet him as he approached. They delighted in selecting their favourite bread and sometimes even chose sweet bread or special varieties. Among their favourites were the bread bangles, which they carefully picked out from the baker's offerings.

**Question 2: What did the bakers wear?**

(i) in the Portuguese days?

(ii) when the author was young?

**Answer:**

(i) In the Portuguese days, the bakers were usually dressed up in a peculiar dress known as the kabai. It was a single-piece long frock reaching down to the knees.

(ii) During the author’s childhood days, he saw the bakers wearing shirts and trousers that were shorter than full-length ones and longer than half pants.

**Question 3: Who invites the comment “he is dressed like a pader”? Why?**

**Answer:** In Goa, it's common for people to affectionately refer to anyone wearing shorts that reach just below the knees as "dressed like a pader". This nickname stems from the attire of the bakers, who are fondly called "paders" in the region. These bakers traditionally sported shorts of a similar length, hence the association with their attire.

**Question 4: Where were the monthly accounts of the baker recorded?**

Answer:

The bakers in Goa had a unique way of managing their finances. Instead of collecting payments daily, they would gather their bills at the end of each month. These bills were then recorded on a wall inside the house using a pencil. This simple yet effective method allowed the bakers to keep track of their monthly accounts and ensure that all transactions were properly documented.

**Question 2: Is bread an important part of Goan life? How do you know this?**

**Answer:** Bread holds significant cultural importance in Goan life, serving as more than just a staple food. It plays a central role in various customs and celebrations, such as being offered as marriage gifts and served during feasts. Mothers even use bread to prepare sandwiches for their daughters' engagements.

The aroma of freshly baked loaves is cherished by everyone in Goa, with elders enjoying traditional loaves and youngsters eagerly seeking out bread-bangles. As a result, having bread on hand for all occasions is considered essential in every household. Given this cultural affinity for tasty bread since the days of Portuguese influence, baking remains a lucrative and respected business in Goa.

## NCERT Solutions for Class 10 English First Flight Chapter 7 FAQs

**Q.1. What is the baker called in Class 10 English First Flight Chapter 7?**

**Ans**. In the story A Baker from Goa, the baker is called a 'pader'. These Goan bakers were an important part of village life. They made fresh loaves of bread and cakes and delivered them to every home daily.

**Q.2. Why does the author describe Coorg as a piece of heaven?**

**Ans.** The author calls Coorg a slice of heaven because of its peaceful hills, evergreen forests, and coffee plantations. The land is full of natural beauty, wildlife, and brave people, which makes it look like a small heaven on Earth.

**Q.3. Where can I get detailed Class 10 English Chapter 7 Glimpses of India question answers?**

**Ans.** You can find all detailed Class 10 English Chapter 7 Glimpses of India question answers and explanations in the article above.

**Q.4. Who are the paders, and why are they liked by children?**

**Ans**. In Goa, village bakers are known as paders. They move around the village selling bread and other bakery items. Children wait for them every morning because they bring tasty bread bangles and sweet loaves that everyone enjoys.

**Q.5. What is the legend behind the discovery of tea in India?**

**Ans.** According to an old Indian legend, a Buddhist monk named Bodhidharma once cut off his eyelids to avoid sleep while meditating. From those eyelids grew tea plants. Later, people found that drinking water made from tea leaves helped them stay awake and fresh.
