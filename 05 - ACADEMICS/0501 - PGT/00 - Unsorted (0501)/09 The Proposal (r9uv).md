## Master Study Note: The Proposal (Anton Chekhov)

This one-act play is a farce that satirizes the landed gentry of 19th-century Russia. It highlights how economic interests and petty ego often override romance and emotional logic. As an examiner, I have focused on the absurd contradictions in the characters' behavior and the repetitive nature of their conflicts.

---

### 1. Character Profiles & Traits

**A. Stepan Stepanovitch Chubukov (The Father)**
*   **Hypocritical & Opportunistic**: He is initially suspicious of Lomov (thinking he’s come to borrow money) but becomes "extremely glad" once he realizes a proposal is coming, as it secures his daughter's future.
*   **Hot-Headed**: He quickly joins the arguments between Lomov and Natalya, escalating the tension rather than acting as a mediator.
*   **Endearingly Fake**: Frequently uses terms of endearment like "my darling," "my beauty," and "angel," even when he is about to insult Lomov.

**B. Ivan Vassilevitch Lomov (The Suitor)**
*   **Hypochondriac**: A 35-year-old bachelor who is "full of palpitations," "excitable," and constantly worried about his sleep and "twitches."
*   **Formal & Pedantic**: He approaches the proposal like a legal transaction, dressed in a dress-jacket and white gloves, obsessed with proving the legality of his property.
*   **Socially Awkward**: He lacks the social grace to steer a conversation toward romance, instead falling into traps of pride and territorial disputes.

**C. Natalya Stepanovna (The Daughter)**
*   **Efficient & Domineering**: She is an excellent housekeeper but possesses a "shrewish" temperament. 
*   **Contradictory**: She fights Lomov tooth and nail over the "Oxen Meadows," but as soon as she hears he came to propose, she falls into hysterics and demands he be brought back.
*   **Stubborn**: Like Lomov, her pride in her family’s "possessions" (land and dogs) far outweighs her desire for a peaceful marriage.

---

### 2. Detailed Plot Chronology (Phases)

#### Phase I: The Formal Arrival (The Exposition)
*   **The Setting**: Chubukov’s country house. Lomov enters in formal evening dress.
*   **The Intent**: After some rambling and nervous stalling, Lomov reveals to Chubukov that he has come to ask for Natalya’s hand in marriage. 
*   **The Blessing**: Chubukov is delighted, kisses Lomov, and calls for Natalya, though he doesn't tell her the reason for Lomov's visit.

#### Phase II: The First Argument (The Inciting Incident)
*   **The Stalling**: Lomov tries to lead up to the proposal by mentioning the long-standing friendly relations between their families.
*   **The Trigger**: He mentions "my Oxen Meadows," which touch the Chubukovs' Birchwoods.
*   **The Conflict**: Natalya interrupts, claiming the Meadows belong to her family. The proposal is entirely forgotten as they argue over ownership, legal deeds, and the "grandmother’s" history.

#### Phase III: The Hysteria (Rising Action)
*   **The Exit**: After a heated exchange involving Chubukov, where they trade insults (calling each other "pettifoggers" and "swindlers"), Lomov leaves in a fit of palpitations.
*   **The Revelation**: Chubukov mentions that the "fool" came to propose.
*   **The U-Turn**: Natalya is shocked. She screams, "Bring him back! I'm dying!" Her territorial pride instantly shifts to a desperate need to get married.

#### Phase IV: The Second Argument (The Climax)
*   **The Re-entry**: Lomov returns, exhausted. Natalya tries to be civil and concedes the Meadows.
*   **The New Trigger**: The conversation shifts to their dogs—Lomov’s **Guess** and Natalya’s **Squeezer**.
*   **The Escalation**: They argue over which dog is superior in speed, jaw structure, and pedigree. Chubukov joins in again, and Lomov eventually collapses from a "nervous breakdown" or fainting spell.

#### Phase V: The "Proposal" (Falling Action)
*   **The Panic**: Chubukov and Natalya think Lomov is dead. They panic, realizing the marriage opportunity is slipping away.
*   **The Awakening**: Lomov regains consciousness. Chubukov hurriedly puts Lomov’s hand into Natalya’s and shouts, "He consents!" and "Hurry up and get married!"

#### Phase VI: The Eternal Conflict (Resolution)
*   **The "Peace"**: They are officially engaged, but within seconds, they resume their argument about the dogs. 
*   **The Irony**: Chubukov calls for "Champagne!" to drown out the noise, realizing that this marriage will be a lifetime of shouting and bickering.

### 3. Symbolism & Imagery

| Symbol/Object | Context | Deeper Meaning |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Oxen Meadows** | The disputed land. | Represents the petty materialism of the landed gentry; land is more important than love. |
| **Dress-Jacket/Gloves** | Lomov’s attire. | Symbolizes the empty formality of the upper class; the "polite" exterior hides a volatile interior. |
| **Palpitations** | Lomov’s physical ailments. | Represents his inner instability and the fragility of his character. |
| **Guess & Squeezer** | The hunting dogs. | Symbolizes family pride and the human tendency to find reasons to feel superior to others. |

---

### 4. Thematic Deep-Dive

*   **Marriage as an Economic Contract**: Chekhov portrays marriage not as a union of souls, but as a merger of properties. The characters are more interested in land and dogs than in each other's personalities.
*   **Petty Pride and Ego**: The characters are willing to sabotage their own happiness to prove they are "right" about trivial matters.
*   **Hypocrisy of the Elite**: The thin veneer of "civilized" behavior (evening dress, formal language) quickly vanishes, revealing a crude, aggressive nature.

---

### 5. Irony, Satire, and Literary Devices

*   **Situational Irony**: Lomov comes to propose a union but ends up creating a deep divide over a few acres of grass.
*   **Dramatic Irony**: The audience knows Lomov is there to propose while Natalya is still treating him like a trespassing neighbor.
*   **Farce**: The play uses exaggerated characters, physical comedy (Lomov’s fainting), and absurd situations to mock social customs.
*   **Hyperbole**: Chubukov’s exaggerated expressions of love and hatred for Lomov.

***Thinking About the Play*** 

***1. What does Chubukov at first suspect that Lomov has come for? Is he sincere when he later says. “And I’ve always loved you, my angel, as if you were my own son”? Find reasons for your answer from the play.*** 

***Ans.*** Chubukov at first suspects that Lomov has come for borrowing money from him. He was not sincere when he told Lomov that he had always loved him as his own son. He treats Lomov rudely, even harshly on small matters such as ownership of worthless Oxen Meadows or superiority of the dogs. He abused him, cursed him, called him doublefaced intriguer, villain, scarecrow, blind hen, turnip ghost and so on. He had decided that he would not give money to Lomov. If he truly meant what he said, then he would not have thought of not giving money to him. 

***2. Chubukov says of Natalya: “...as if she won’t consent! She’s in love; egad, she’s like a lovesick cat ....” Would you agree? Find reasons for your answer.*** 

***Ans.*** I would agree that she is in love. She is a love-sick cat. The following reasons from the play support it. She is happy to see and receive Lomov. When Lomov goes away and Natalya learns that he came here to propose her, she asks her father to bring him back. When Lomov is unconscious, she hysterically repeats, “A doctor! A doctor! He’s dead.” This reflects her love for Lomov. OR Chubukov through that Lomov was a good marriage prospect for his daughter. He had been waiting for this proposal. When Lomov expressed his doubt regarding Natalya’s consent, Chubukov immediately told him that she was in love with him. However, this was not true. Natalya did not seem to be in love with him at any point in the play. It seemed like she was more attached to her land, meadows and dogs than to Lomov. In fact, the way they kept getting into arguments about trivial matters suggests that neither of them were in love with each other.
