# M. K. Gandhi

* **Key Terms Associated With**:
    * **Satyagraha**: The philosophy and practice of non-violent resistance; central to Gandhi’s political and ethical thought[6][7].
    * **Ahimsa (Non-violence)**: Commitment to non-violence in thought, word, and deed; a guiding principle in his life and writings[6][7].
    * **Truth (Satya)**: The pursuit of truth as the highest duty; truth and non-violence are inseparable in Gandhi’s philosophy[6][7].
    * **Swaraj (Self-rule)**: Advocacy for political, spiritual, and moral autonomy for individuals and the nation[6][7].
    * **Constructive Programme**: Emphasis on grassroots reforms-village industries, education, health, sanitation, and self-reliance-as the foundation for national regeneration[6][7].
    * **Village Economy & Swadeshi**: Promotion of rural self-sufficiency, khadi (handspun cloth), and local industry as means to empower India[6][7].
    * **Religious Pluralism**: Deep respect for all religions; writings reflect themes of tolerance, unity, and interfaith harmony[6][7].
    * **Moral & Social Reform**: Advocacy for upliftment of the marginalized, women’s rights, and eradication of untouchability[6][7].
    * **Autobiographical Honesty**: Candid self-examination and confession, especially in his autobiography[6][7].

## Major Works

- **An Autobiography: The Story of My Experiments with Truth** (1927)
    - Gandhi’s most famous work; details his personal, spiritual, and political journey, and the evolution of his philosophy of truth and non-violence[6][7][8].
- **Hind Swaraj or Indian Home Rule** (1909)
    - A foundational text critiquing modern civilization and colonialism; advocates for self-rule, moral regeneration, and traditional Indian values[6][7][8].
- **Satyagraha in South Africa** (1928)
    - Chronicles Gandhi’s activism in South Africa and the development of Satyagraha as a method of resistance[7][8].
- **Constructive Programme: Its Meaning and Place** (1941)
    - Outlines Gandhi’s vision for social and economic reform through grassroots action and community self-reliance[6][7].
- **Key to Health** (1948)
    - Practical guide to health, diet, and hygiene, reflecting Gandhi’s holistic approach to well-being[7].
- **India of My Dreams** (1947)
    - Compilation of Gandhi’s vision for a free, just, and self-reliant India[6][7].
- **From Yeravda Mandir** (1932)
    - Letters from prison, discussing religion, ethics, and social issues[7].
- **Truth is God** (1955, posthumous)
    - Collection of Gandhi’s writings on the centrality of truth in his philosophy[7].
- **My Religion** (1957, posthumous)
    - Gandhi’s thoughts on religion, non-violence, and faith[7].
- **Village Swaraj** (2012, collection)
    - Writings on village self-rule and rural development[1].
- **The Bhagavad Gita According to Gandhi**
    - Gandhi’s interpretation and commentary on the Gita’s teachings[1].
- **Collected Works of Mahatma Gandhi**
    - Comprehensive 90+ volume set of Gandhi’s letters, speeches, and writings[4][8].

## Other Notable Aspects

- **Journalism**: Edited journals like *Young India*, *Harijan*, and *Navjivan*, using them as platforms for social and political reform.
- **Correspondence & Letters**: Extensive letters to political leaders, followers, and adversaries, reflecting his transparent and dialogic approach[3][4].
- **Legacy**: Gandhi’s writings remain foundational for studies in peace, conflict resolution, ethics, and political science.
