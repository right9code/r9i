# Sources of Anglo Saxon Prose

## Religious Prose: Homilies (Sermons) & Hagiography (Saints' Lives)
- **Catholic Homilies (I & II)**: Ælfric of Eynsham 
	- **Author**: Ælfric of Eynsham (c. 955–c. 1010)
	- **Source**: First Series: Cambridge, Corpus Christi College; Second Series: Oxford, Bodleian Library, Laud Misc. 382
	- **Content:** These are two collections of sermons (homilies) composed by Ælfric in the 990s.
		- **First Series (I)** : Completed c. 990.
			- 40 homilies covering major feast days (Christmas, Easter, Pentecost) and Sundays
		- **Second Series (II)**: Written shortly after, c. 992–995
			- 40 homilies expanding on doctrine, saints’ lives, and eschatology (e.g., Judgment Day)
	- **Rejects apocrypha**[^1]: Corrects earlier homilies like the _Blickling Homilies_ by adhering strictly to patristic sources (Gregory, Augustine, Jerome).
- **Lives of Saints**: Ælfric of Eynsham
	- **Author**: Ælfric of Eynsham (c. 955–c. 1010)
	- **Source**: British Library Cotton Julius E.vii
	- **Content**: contains over 30 hagiographies.
- **Sermo Lupi ad Anglos**: (Sermon of the Wolf to the English) – Wulfstan
	- **Author**: Wulfstan II, Archbishop of York (d. 1023), also Bishop of Worcester and London.
	- **Source**: BL Cotton Nero A.i: Contains Wulfstan’s handwritten revisions, dated to 1014
	- **Content**: A fiery Old English sermon blaming societal sins (greed, treachery, sacrilege) for Viking invasions and natural disasters. It warns of divine retribution and impending apocalypse, urging repentance to avert further punishment.
	- Written around 1014 by Archbishop Wulfstan during devastating Viking raids. 
- **Institutes of Polity**: (Gerecednes Rihtes) Wulfstan
	- **Author**: Wulfstan II, Archbishop of York (d. 1023), also Bishop of Worcester and London.
	- **Source**: London, British Library, Cotton MS Nero A.i (ff. 70–177)
	- **Content**: It is a more systematic treatise outlining the structure of an ideal Christian society based on divinely ordained social orders (ordines) and the corresponding duties of each group.
	- Outlines duties for all societal ranks (king to peasant), blending law, theology, and moral reform.
	- in four sections.  
- **Blickling Homilies**: Anonymous
	- **Author**: Anonymous
	- **Source**: Princeton University Library, Scheide Library MS 71 (formerly in Blickling Hall, Norfolk, hence the name)
	- **Content**: A collection of eighteen anonymous Old English homilies and one fragment.
		- Sermons for specific feast days (e.g., Annunciation, Assumption of Mary, Ascension, Pentecost, All Saints), lives or passions of saints (e.g., St. Andrew, St. Martin), General moral and theological themes etc.
	- The Blickling Homilies are the earliest surviving Anglo-Saxon homiliary written entirely in the vernacular
- **Vercelli Homilies**: Anonymous
	- **Author**: Anonymous
	- **Source**: Vercelli Book (Vercelli, Biblioteca Capitolare MS CXVII)
	- **Content**: A collection of 23 Old English prose sermons.  
- **Life of St Chad (prose)**: Anonymous
	- **Author**: Anonymous
	- **Source**: British Library, Cotton MS Vespasian D XXI
	- **Contents**: The work narrates the life of St Guthlac of Crowland.
		- A Mercian noble who became a monk and later a hermit in the Lincolnshire Fens (Crowland).
	- An adaptation of the Latin Vita sancti Guthlaci by Felix.
	- It's important to distinguish this prose version from the Old English poems about Guthlac (Guthlac A and Guthlac B), which are found in the Exeter Book.
- **Life of St. Mary of Egypt**: Anonymous (fragmentary)
	- **Author**: Anonymous
	- **Source**: Cotton Julius E.vii (British Library)
	- **Content**: After a youth of extreme sin as a prostitute, Mary of Egypt undergoes a dramatic conversion in Jerusalem, prompted by the Virgin Mary after being barred from the Holy Sepulchre. Repenting, she crosses the Jordan and lives 47 years as an intensely ascetic desert hermit. Near death, she recounts her life to the monk Zosimus, receives communion, and dies. Zosimus later finds her body identified by her name miraculously written beside it.  
- **Life of St. Margaret**: Anonymous (prose version)
	- **Author**: Anonymous
	- **Source**: BL Cotton Tiberius A.iii
	- **Content**: The work narrates the story of **St. Margaret of Antioch**, a virgin martyr who rejected a pagan official(Olybrius, the governor of Antioch), endured torture, famously battled a dragon and demon, and was ultimately beheaded for her steadfast Christian faith.
- **Old English Martyrology**: Anonymous (fragmentary calendar of saints)
	- **Author**: Anonymous
	- **Source**: British Library Cotton Julius A.x
	- **Content**: A collection of short prose accounts of saints’ lives, arranged according to the Christian calendar.  

## Religious Prose: Biblical & Patristic/Theological Translations
- **West Saxon Gospels**: Anonymous OE[^2] translation  
	- **Author**: Anonymous 
	- **Source**: survives in 8 manuscripts; Cambridge, Corpus Christi College MS 140
	- **Content**: This is a translation of the four canonical Gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John) from the New Testament into the Old English language.
- **Old English Hexateuch**: Ælfric of Eynsham (with collaborators)
	- **Author**: Ælfric of Eynsham (with collaborators)
	- **Source**:British Library Cotton MS Claudius B.iv
	- **Contents**: The earliest surviving English vernacular translation of the first six books of the Old Testament: Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy, and Joshua.   
- **Pastoral Care**: King Alfred's OE translation of Gregory
	- **Author**: King Alfred's OE translation of Gregory
	- **Source**: at least six manuscripts survive; Oxford, Bodleian Library, MS Hatton 20
	- **Content**: Gregory's Pastoral Care is a seminal work on the responsibilities and duties of bishops.
	- particularly famous for the Preface written by King Alfred himself.   
- **Dialogues**: Bishop Werferth's OE translation of Gregory
	- **Author**: Bishop Werferth's OE translation of Gregory
	- **Source**: Cambridge, Corpus Christi College, MS 322
	- **Content**: Gregory's Dialogues consists of four books, structured as a conversation between Gregory and his deacon, Peter. The work primarily recounts the lives and miracles of holy men (mostly monks and bishops) in Italy during the 6th century.  
- **Ecclesiastical History of English People**: (Historia Ecclesiastica Gentis Anglorum) Alfredian OE translation of Bede
	- **Author**: Alfredian OE translation of Bede
	- **Source**: Bodleian Library, Oxford, MS Tanner 10
	- **Content**: Bede’s original Latin work (completed c. 731) chronicles the Christianization and history of the English people from Roman times to Bede’s own day.
- **Soliloquies**: King Alfred's OE adaptation of Augustine
	- **Author**: King Alfred's OE adaptation of Augustine
	- **Source**: British Library, Cotton MS Vitellius A.xv
	- **Content**: A philosophical dialogue in two books, primarily between Augustine himself and Reason (Ratio). It explores fundamental questions about self-knowledge, the nature of truth, the immortality of the soul, and the desire to know God.
- **Rule of St. Benedict**: Partial OE translation of Regula Benedict
	- **Author**: St. Æthelwold of Winchester's partial translation of St. Benedict of Nursia
	- **Source**: Cotton Titus A.IV and Faustina A.X, part B
	- **Content**:The Rule provides comprehensive guidelines for monastic life. 
	- The first vernacular translation of the Rule of St. Benedict
- **Paris Psalter**: Prose translation of Psalms 1–50
	- **Author**: Anonymous
	- **Source**: Bibliothèque Nationale, Paris, MS fonds latin 8824
	- **Content**: The Old English prose translation of the first fifty Psalms.
		- Each page presents the Latin text in one column and the Old English translation in the other, allowing for direct comparison.  
	- The verse translation (Psalms 51-150) employs the techniques of Old English poetry, including alliteration and traditional poetic vocabulary, resulting in a more literary adaptation.
- **Confessional Prayers (Scriftboc)**: Anonymous penitential instructions
	- **Author**: Anonymous
	- **Source**: Corpus Christi College, Cambridge, MS 190
	- **Content**: Confessional prayers-set forms for penitents to confess their sins.

## Historical & Chronological Prose
- **The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle**: Multiple scribes
	- **Author**: Multiple scribes
	- **Source**: 
		- The Parker Chronicle (Corpus Christi College, Cambridge, MS 173) - Manuscript A, the oldest surviving manuscript.
		- The Laud Chronicle (Bodleian Library, Oxford, MS Laud Misc 636) - Manuscript E, the Peterborough Chronicle, continued the latest.
	- **Content**:  it is a collection of annals, meaning it records events year by year.
		- Compilation likely began during the reign of King Alfred the Great (late 9th century), possibly as part of his program to promote learning and English identity. 
		- Final Entry: The very last entry in the Peterborough Chronicle (and thus the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle as a whole) records events up to the death of King Stephen and the accession of Henry II in 1154.
- **Histories Against Pagans**: adapted OE translation of Orosius
	- **Author**: OE translation of Paulus Orosius, a priest and historian from Gallaecia (Roman Spain), who wrote the Latin original in the early 5th century (completed around 417/418 AD), likely at the suggestion of his teacher, St. Augustine of Hippo.
	- **Source**: The Lauderdale MS or Tollemache Orosius
	- **Content**: It covers world history from Creation to Orosius’s own time (early 5th century), focusing on the Mediterranean and Near East but expanding to include other regions, especially through additions in the Old English version. 
		- It is divided into six books, each further subdivided into sections.
		- additions includes Voyages of Ohthere and Wulfstan 
	- **Old English Heptateuch**: Hexateuch + Book of Joshua (sometimes distinguished)
		- **Author**: Ælfric of Eynsham, who collaborated on parts of the Hexateuch translation, also produced a separate translation/adaptation of the Book of Judges
		- **Source**: Oxford, Bodleian Library, Laud Misc. 509
		- **Content**: the vernacular translation of the first seven books of the Old Testament (Genesis through Judges) 

## Legal Prose
- **Law Codes**:
	- **Source**: Textus Roffensis, CCCC MS 383, BL Cotton MS Nero A I, etc.
	- **Content**: 
		- **Æthelberht of Kent (c. 602)**:
		- Earliest surviving Anglo-Saxon law code and the oldest in any Germanic language.
		- Focuses on compensation (wergild) for injuries, theft, and offenses against church, clergy, and social ranks.
		- **Hlothhere and Eadric of Kent (c. 673–685)**:
			- Addressed judicial procedures, oaths, and compensation for wrongs.
		- **Wihtred of Kent (695)**:
			- regarding church rights and religious observance including provisions on Sabbath observance and church privileges.
		- **Ine of Wessex (c. 688–694)**:
			- First major law code outside Kent
			- Covered property rights, social status, land tenure, and the treatment of slaves.
		- **Offa of Mercia (r. 757–796)**:
			- Issued a law code, now lost, but referenced by later kings.
		- **Alfred the Great (c. 890)**:
			- Compiled and revised earlier laws into his own code, the “Doom Book".
			- His code’s prologue draws on the Bible and penitentials, marking the first attempt at a uniform legal system in England
		- **Edward the Elder, Athelstan, Edmund, Edgar (10th century)**:
			- Addressed issues like public order, administration, and the evolving relationship between church and state.
		- **Æthelred the Unready (late 10th–early 11th century)**:
			- Issued law codes reflecting increasing royal authority and Christian moral concerns.
			- Included measures for public security and penalties for treason and rebellion.
		- **Cnut the Great (early 11th century)**:
			- Last major Anglo-Saxon law codes before the Norman Conquest, remained influential even after 1066. 
- **Charters** – Land grants and legal agreements  
- **Writs** – Administrative orders  
- **Wills** – Testaments and property transfers  

## Philosophical & Educational Prose
- **Consolation of Philosophy**: King Alfred's OE adaptation of Boethius
	- **Author**: King Alfred's OE adaptation of Boethius
	- **Source**:Bodleian Library, Oxford, MS Bodley 180 
	- **Content**: In dialogue with Lady Philosophy, the imprisoned Boethius seeks consolation by exploring true vs. false happiness, fate, divine Providence, free will, foreknowledge, and the problem of evil, aiming to understand the divine order through reason. 
- **Grammar**: Ælfric's Latin-Old English guide
	- **Author**: Ælfric of Eynsham
	- **Source**: St John's College, Oxford, MS 154
	- **Content**: The first Latin grammar written in a European vernacular-Old English-created around 995 to teach Latin to Anglo-Saxon novices with little or no prior exposure to the language.
- **Colloquy** – Ælfric's Latin-Old English dialogue
	- **Author**: Ælfric of Eynsham
	- **Source** British Library, Cotton MS Tiberius A iii
	- **Content**: It is a Latin-Old English educational dialogue composed to help novice monks and schoolchildren learn Latin through conversational practice.
		- Written around the turn of the 11th century, the Colloquy takes the form of a dialogue between a schoolmaster and his pupils, each representing different occupations such as ploughman, shepherd, hunter, merchant, and others. 
- **Byrhtferth's Enchiridion / Handboc** – Scientific/computistical manual (mixed OE/Latin)
	- **Author**: Byrhtferth, a monk and scholar associated with Ramsey Abbey.
	- **Source**: Bodleian Library, Oxford, MS Ashmole 328.
	- **Content**: This is a complex and learned scholarly manual primarily focused on computus – the intricate science of calculating the calendar, particularly the date of Easter.
		- Expands into arithmology (symbolic meanings of numbers), astronomy, rhetoric, grammar, and metrics, reflecting the medieval curriculum’s encyclopedic scope.
		- Includes schemata (charts) to visualize computistical cycles, such as the 19-year Metonic cycle and the 28-year solar cycle, often annotated with Old English explanations.
- **De Temporibus Anni** – Ælfric's treatise on timekeeping and cosmology  
	- **Author**: Ælfric of Eynsham (c. 955 – c. 1010).
	- **Source**: Cambridge University Library, MS Gg. 3. 28
	- **Content**: This work is an accessible treatise written entirely in Old English that explains basic cosmology, astronomy, basic meteorology and computus (time calculation) as understood in his time.

## Scientific, Medical, & Practical Prose
- **Bald's Leechbook**: Medical compendium
	- **Author**: Anonymous.
		- However, a Latin colophon (inscription) at the end of Book II mentions a Latin colophon stating, “Bald owns this book, which he ordered Cild to write”
	- **Source**: British Library, Royal MS 12 D xvii
	- **Content**: The Leechbook medical compendium is divided into three books.
		- **Book I** addresses external maladies, arranged head-to-foot, and includes treatments for wounds, skin diseases, and injuries.
		- **Book II** covers internal disorders, such as fevers and digestive complaints, also in head-to-foot order-a structural approach unique among medieval medical texts.
		- **Book III** is somewhat independent and reflects distinctly Anglo-Saxon medical practices, less influenced by Mediterranean source.
	- Generally divided into two main books (Book I and Book II). A third section, often referred to as *Leechbook III*, follows in the same manuscript but appears to be a distinct collection of recipes, possibly compiled separately.
- **Lacnunga** ("Remedies"): Charms and recipes
	- **Author**: Anonymous
	- **Source**: British Library, Royal MS 12 D xvii
	- **Content**:  This is a fascinating and somewhat eclectic collection of medical recipes, prayers, and magical charms. 
- **Old English Herbarium**: Translation of Pseudo-Apuleius
	- **Author**: Anonymous.
	- **Source**: British Library, Cotton MS Vitellius C iii
	- **Content**:  The Herbarium describes over 100 plants (herbs) and their medicinal properties.
- **Lapidaries**: Descriptions of stones (often appended to Herbarium)
	- **Author**:Anonymous.
	- **Source**: British Library, Cotton MS Vitellius C iii
	- **Content**: Typically describe individual stones (e.g., diamond, sapphire, emerald, agate, jet, pearl) and and their properties.
- **Medicina de Quadrupedibus**: Animal-based remedies
	- **Author**: Anonymous.
	- **Source**: British Library, Harley MS 585
	- **Content**: For various animals (e.g., hare, wolf, goat, bull, lion, elephant - some exotic, known only through textual tradition), it describes how different parts of their bodies or their by-products could be used to treat specific ailments.
- **Prognostics**: Texts for predicting weather, health, or fate
	- **Author**: Anonymous.
	- **Source**: British Library, Cotton Tiberius A.iii
	- **Content**: A diverse group of texts-about forty to fifty items in Latin and Old English-designed to predict aspects of weather, health, fate, and daily affairs. 
		- Rather than using astrology, these texts base their predictions on the cycles of days, months, and years, drawing on calendrical and natural phenomena

## Literary Prose (Narrative & Marvels)
- **Apollonius of Tyre**: OE translation of Greek romance
	- **Author**: Anonymous
	- **Source**: Corpus Christi College, Cambridge, MS 201
	- **Content**: A prose romance narrative telling the complex story of Apollonius, the king of Tyre.
		-  King Apollonius solves a deadly riddle revealing a king's incest, forcing him into exile to escape revenge. During his travels filled with shipwrecks and adventures, he marries a princess. Tragically, she seemingly dies giving birth on a sea voyage and is set adrift in a chest (but secretly survives). Apollonius leaves his newborn daughter with guardians, who later plot against her. After many years of suffering, separation, and further perils for his daughter (including pirates), through a series of remarkable coincidences, Apollonius is miraculously reunited first with his daughter and then with his long-lost wife.
	- directly translated from a popular Latin prose version, known as the Historia Apollonii regis Tyri.
- **Letter of Alexander to Aristotle**: Marvels from the Beowulf Manuscript
	- **Author**: Anonymous
	- **Source**: British Library, Cotton Vitellius A.xv (Beowulf MS)
	- **Content**: The letter is structured as a fictional travel narrative in which Alexander recounts his journey through India and the East.
		- He describes encounters with a host of fabulous and monstrous creatures-giant snakes, men with dogs’ heads, headless people, and other marvels-many of which are echoed in the Wonders of the East, another text in the same manuscript.
- **Wonders of the East**: Descriptions of monsters and wonders
	- **Author**: Anonymous
	- **Source**: British Library, Cotton MS Vitellius A.xv (Beowulf MS)
	- **Content**: A fictional catalogue of marvels, monsters, and extraordinary peoples said to inhabit the distant lands of the East-Babylonia, Egypt, India, and beyond 

## Other Liturgical & Monastic Prose
- **Rule of Chrodegang** – OE adaptation of Frankish monastic rule (fragmentary)
	- **Author**:  Anonymous adaptation of St. Chrodegang
	- **Source**: Corpus Christi College, Cambridge, MS 178
	- **Content**: The translation is fragmentary but closely follows the Latin "Enlarged Rule," which consists of 86 chapters in the Aachen recension.
		- It covers topics such as communal discipline, liturgical observance, forms of address, spiritual conduct, and the practical organization of clerical life.
	- St. Chrodegang, Bishop of Metz (in Francia, modern France) wrote the Latin rule around 755 AD.
- **Monastic Rules** – Fragmentary OE adaptations (e.g., for Winchester)
	- **Author**: Various
	- **Source**: Various
	- **Content**: refers not to a single defined work but to various fragmentary texts, adaptations, excerpts, or supplementary regulations related to monastic life, written in Old English. They often supplement or adapt the main Rule of St. Benedict for specific contexts.
		- e.g. Rule of St. Basil, Rule of St. Augustine, etc.
		- Winchester, particularly under Bishop Æthelwold, was a major center of the Benedictine Reform.
