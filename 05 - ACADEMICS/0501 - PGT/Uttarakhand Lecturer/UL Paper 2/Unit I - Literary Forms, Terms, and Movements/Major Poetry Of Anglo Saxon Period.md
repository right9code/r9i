# Major Poetry Of Anglo Saxon Period

## Beowulf

- **Name**: Beowulf
- **Author**: Anonymous
- **Genre**: Epic poem, heroic verse.
- **Language**: composed in Anglian dialect and written in West Saxon dialect.
- **Length**: 3182 lines.
- **Source**: Second Codex of Nowell Codex,Cotton Vitellius A.xv (Beowulf MS)
- **Date**: 700 - 1000 CE
	- Gregory of Tours that battle in which Hygelac died took place in 520 CE. Since Hygelac is mentioned as Beowulf's uncle it was not composed before 520 CE as Beowulf is loyal warrior under his uncle and after his death.
	- As the poem shows Scandinavian in sympathy and was composed in Anglian dialect when Danish influence was strong i.e. around 725 CE.
	- So it was  thought to be composed between 520 - 725 CE.
	- Today, the more accepted range is c. 700-1000 CE.
- **Key Characters**:
	- **Beowulf**: The epic hero; a Geatish (southern Sweden) warrior known for his strength, courage, and loyalty. He embodies the heroic code.
	- **Hrothgar**: The aging King of the Danes, wise and respected but unable to deal with Grendel.
	- **Grendel**: Monster descended from Cain, represents evil and exclusion.
	- **Grendel's Mother**: A monstrous creature seeking vengeance for her son.
	- **The Dragon**: Ancient creature, symbolizes greed and destruction, causes Beowulf's death.
	- **Wiglaf**: Loyal retainer, helps Beowulf against the dragon, symbolizes loyalty and the future of the heroic tradition.
- **Structure of Poem**:
	- The poem can be divided into two parts:
		- **Part 1**: lines 1 - 2199.
			- King Hrothgar of the Danes builds a magnificent mead-hall called Heorot. However, it's plagued by Grendel, a monstrous descendant of Cain, who attacks nightly, killing and devouring Hrothgar's warriors. 
			- Beowulf, a young, incredibly strong hero from Geatland, hears of Hrothgar's plight, sails to Denmark with his men, and offers his help. 
			- **Battle with Grendel**: He decides to fight Grendel unarmed and waits in Heorot. When Grendel attacks, Beowulf grapples with him, eventually tearing off the monster's arm. Grendel flees to his swampy lair to die.
			- **Battle with Grendel's Mother**: Grendel's mother, seeking revenge for her son's death, attacks Heorot, killing one of Hrothgar's chief advisors. Beowulf tracks her to her lair beneath a murky lake. He dives in, wearing armor and carrying a sword (Hrunting), but the sword proves useless against her. In a desperate underwater battle, he finds a giant's sword in her cave and uses it to kill her. He then finds Grendel's corpse and decapitates it, bringing the head back to Heorot as a trophy.
		- **Part 2**: lines 2200 - 3182.
			- Many years later, Beowulf has returned to Geatland and become a wise and respected king. When a slave steals a jeweled cup from a dragon's hoard, the enraged dragon begins to terrorize Beowulf's kingdom.
			- **Battle with the Dragon**: Now an old man, Beowulf decides he must fight the dragon himself. He attacks the dragon's lair with his retainers, but all except one, the loyal Wiglaf, flee in terror. Beowulf's sword (Nægling) breaks against the dragon's scales. Both Beowulf and Wiglaf manage to wound the dragon, but Beowulf receives a mortal bite.
			- As he dies, Beowulf names Wiglaf his successor and asks for a barrow to be built on a headland in his memory. 
			- The poem ends with Beowulf's funeral pyre and lamentations for the great king.
- **Style**:
	- Alliterative poetry.
		- **Line 3**:
			- **Old English**: þara **æ**þelinga || **e**llen fremedon!
			- **Phonetic Respelling**: THAH-rah ATH-el-ing-gah || EL-len FREM-eh-don!
			- **Translation:**: "how those princes || performed deeds of courage!"
			- **Alliteration**: The vowel sounds æ and e in **æ**þelinga (princes) and **e**llen (courage) alliterate. (In Old English metrics, any vowel sound could alliterate with any other vowel sound). 
			- Links the princes directly to their defining quality: courage.
		- **Line 4**:    
		    - **Old English**: Oft **Sc**yld **Sc**efing || **sc**eaþena þreatum
		    - **Phonetic Respelling**: Oft SHild SHAY-ving || SHAH-theh-nah THRAY-ah-toom
		    - **Translation**: "Often Scyld Scefing (Shield, son/descendant of Scef/Sheaf) || (took mead-benches) from enemy hosts"
		    - **Alliteration**: The sc sound in **Sc**yld, **Sc**efing, and **sc**eaþena alliterate. 
		    - Emphasizes the subject (Scyld Scefing) and the object of his actions (enemy hosts).
		- **Line 6**:
			- **Old English**: **S**yððan ǣrest wearð || **f**ea**sc**eaft **f**unden;
			- **Phonetic Respelling**: SIDH-than AY-rest wahrth || FAY-ah-shaft FUHN-den;
			- **Translation**: "since first he was || found destitute;" (referring to Scyld Scefing)
			- **Alliteration**: The f sound in **f**easceaft (destitute) and **f**unden (found) alliterates. 
			- (Note: While Syððan is stressed, the primary alliterating sound linking across the caesura here is f on the first stressed syllable of the second half-line, linking with another stressed syllable in the second half-line. Metrical patterns can vary). 
			- Links the state of being 'destitute' with the action of being 'found', emphasizing Scyld's humble origins.
		- **Line 115**:
		    - **Old English**: **G**ewat ða **n**eosian, || syþðan **n**iht becom,
		    - **Phonetic Respelling**: Yeh-WAHT DHAH NAY-oh-see-ahn, || SIDH-than nikht beh-KOHM,
		    - **Translation**: "Then went he to investigate, || after **n**ight had come,"        
		    - **Alliteration**: The n sound in **n**eosian (investigate/visit) and **n**iht (night) alliterate across the caesura. 
		    - Links the action of investigating with the time it occurred.
		- **Line 127**:
			- **Old English**: **þ**a wæs **æ**fter wiste || **w**op up ahafen
			- **Phonetic Respelling**: Thah wahs AF-ter WIS-teh || wohp oop ah-HAH-ven
		    - **Translation**: "Then after the feast || **w**ailing was raised up"
		    - **Alliteration**: The w sound in **w**iste (feast) and **w**op (wailing) alliterate. 
		    - Creates a stark contrast between the joy of the feast and the sorrow that immediately followed.
	- Opening with great events of past and describing kings of past:
		- entire prologue of the poem (approximately lines 1-52), which focuses entirely on Scyld Scefing, the legendary founder of the Danish royal dynasty (the Scyldings).
	- **Use of Kennings**: (metaphorical compound expressions)
		- **heaþoswāt** (HATH-oh-swaht) - battle-sweat: blood
		- **hronrād** (HRON-rahd) - whale-road: the sea
		- **bēaggifa** (BAY-ahg-yif-ah) - ring-giver: king
		- **rodores candel** (ROD-or-es KAN-del) - sky-candle: the sun
		- **wældrēor** (WAHL-dray-or) - slaughter-dew: blood
		- **gār-ræs** (GAHR-rass) - spear-din/spear-rush: battle
		- **sǣ-mearh** (SAY-marh) - sea-steed: ship
		- **gūð-fȳr** (GOOTH-feer) - battle-fire: dragon’s flaming breath
	- **Use of Caesura (||)**: (a deliberate pause or break within a line of poetry, usually marked by punctuation)
		- **Line 1**: 
			- **Old English**: Hwæt! We Gardena || in geardagum, 
			- **Phonetic Respelling**: Hwaht! Way GAR-day-nah || in YAR-dah-goom,
			- **Translation**: "Listen! We of the Spear-Danes || in days of yore,"
		- **Line 4**: 
			- **Old English**: Oft Scyld Scefing || sceaþena þreatum 
			- **Phonetic Respelling**: Oft SHild SHAY-ving || SHAH-theh-nah THRAY-ah-toom
			- **Translation**: "Often Scyld Scefing (Shield, son/descendant of Scef/Sheaf) || (took mead-benches) from enemy hosts"
		- **Line 115**:
			- **Old English**: Gewat ða neosian, || syþðan niht becom,
			- **Phonetic Respelling**: Yeh-WAHT DHAH NAY-oh-see-ahn, || SIDH-than nikht beh-KOHM,
			- **Translation**: "Then went he to investigate, || after night had come,"
		- Caesura typically divides each line into two balanced halves, supporting the poem’s alliterative structure and making it easier for oral recitation.
	- **Use of Formulas**: (repeated phrases or sentence structures characteristic of oral poetry)
		- **maþelode** (“spoke”): Used repeatedly to introduce speech, e.g., “Beowulf maþelode” (“Beowulf spoke”)
			- *Character Name* maþelode, || bearn *Father's Name/Epithet*
		- **wordhord onleac** (“unlocked his word-hoard”): A formulaic way to say someone began to speak
		- **andswarode** (“answered”): Used to introduce a reply.
	- **Christian Ideas**: 
		- Grendel is associated with biblical Cain.
- **Key Old English Concepts**:
	- **Wyrd**: (Fate, Destiny)
		- Beowulf acknowledges Wyrd but acts with courage regardless.
		- His famous line, "Wyrd oft nereð / unfægne eorl, þonne his ellen deah!" (approx. "Fate often spares an undoomed warrior when his courage is strong!")
	- **Lof**: (Praise, Glory, Renown)
		- Beowulf is explicitly driven by the desire for lof. 
		- He travels to Denmark seeking glory, fights monsters partly for the renown it brings.
		- His final wish is for a barrow to be built as a monument, ensuring his deeds (and thus his lof) are remembered.
- **Critic**:
	- **W.P. Ker**: (in Epic and Romance, 1897) famously criticized its structure, finding the plot "thin" and the central conflicts (monsters) less important than the potentially historical digressions.
	- **J R R Tolkien**: In his 1936 lecture/essay emphasized it to be appreciated as work of art then history in exploration of heroism and mortality.
	- **Harold Bloom**: opine that it is not a Christian epic as it does not mention New Testament and there are interpolations done by clerics.
	- **John Gardner**: published 'Grendel' in 1971 from the perspective of Grendal.
	- **Seamus Heaney**: published a translation in 1999.

- **Important Qoutes**:
	1. **The Opening Invocation**:
	    - **Line:** 1
	    - **Old English**: Hwæt! We Gardena || in geardagum,
	    - **Translation**: "Listen! We have heard of the glory of the Spear-Danes in days gone by," (Heaney: "So. The Spear-Danes in days gone by...")
	    - **Significance**: Establishes the epic scope, calls the audience to attention, and grounds the story in legendary history. The famous "Hwæt!" (Listen/Hark/So!) is iconic.
        
	2. **Beowulf's Resolve (Fighting Grendel Unarmed)**:
	    - **Lines**: 435-439 (approx.)
	    - **Old English**: ...ic þæt þonne forhicge, ... þæt ic sweord bere ... ac ic mid grape sceal / fon wið feonde ond ymb feorh sacan, / lað wið laþum...  
	    - **Translation**: "...I therefore scorn... to bear sword... but I with grasp shall grapple with the fiend and contend for life, foe against foe..."
	    - **Significance**: Shows Beowulf's supreme heroic confidence, his desire for fair combat (as Grendel uses no weapons), and his adherence to the heroic code of courage and strength.
        
	3. **Fate and Courage**:
	    - **Lines**: 572b-573        
	    - **Old English**: Wyrd oft nereð / unfægne eorl, þonne his ellen deah!
	    - **Translation**: "Fate often spares an undoomed warrior when his courage holds!"
	    - **Significance**: Perhaps the most famous line encapsulating the relationship between fate (Wyrd) and heroic action. It suggests that while fate is powerful, courage is the proper response and may influence one's survival if not already fated to die.
        
	4. **Hrothgar's Warning Against Pride (Part of his "Sermon")**:
	    - **Lines**: 1760b-1761a 
	    - **Old English**: oferhyda ne gym, / mære cempa!
	    - **Translation**: "Do not give way to pride, famous warrior!"
	    - **Significance**: After Beowulf's victories, the wise King Hrothgar warns him against hubris, reminding him of the transience of power and life. It adds depth and foreshadows the challenges of leadership and mortality Beowulf will face later.
        
	5. **The Impermanence of Earthly Life**:
	    - **Lines**: 2265b-2266 (The "Lay of the Last Survivor")
	    - **Old English**: Heald þu nu, hruse, nu hæleð ne mostan, / eorla æhte!
	    - **Translation**: "Hold now, earth, now that heroes may not, the possessions of earls!"
	    - **Significance**: Spoken by the last survivor of a forgotten race guarding the dragon's hoard, this captures the poem's strong elegiac tone – the sorrow over the passing of time, heroes, and civilizations.
        
	6. **Wiglaf's Loyalty and Rebuke to Cowards**:    
	    - **Lines**: 2890b-2891
	    - **Old English**: Deað bið sella / eorla gehwylcum þonne edwitlif!
	    - **Translation**: "Death is better for any warrior than a life of shame!"
	    - **Significance**: As Beowulf dies, Wiglaf condemns the retainers who fled. This line powerfully affirms the core value of the heroic code: loyalty unto death is preferable to cowardly survival and the resulting disgrace.
        
	7. **Beowulf's Final Wish (Remembrance)**:
	    - **Lines**: 2802-2808 (approx.)
	    - **Old English**: Hatað heaðomære hlæw gewyrcean... æfter Lleges bryne... þæt hit sæliðend syððan hatan / Biowulfes biorh...
	    - **Translation**: "Command the battle-renowned ones to build a mound... after the funeral fire... that seafarers hereafter may call it Beowulf's Barrow..."
	    - **Significance**: Shows the importance of lof (praise, glory) and remembrance. Beowulf seeks immortality through his reputation and a physical monument to ensure his deeds are not forgotten.
        
	8. **The Final Lines Summarizing Beowulf**:
	    - **Lines**: 3180-3182  
	    - **Old English**: cwædon þæt he wære wyruldcyninga / manna mildust ond monðwærust, / leodum liðost ond lofgeornost.  
	    - **Translation**: "...said that he was of world-kings the mildest of men and the gentlest, kindest to his people and most eager for fame."
	    - **Significance**: The poem's final assessment of its hero. It balances his kingly virtues (mildness, gentleness, kindness to his people) with the driving force of the heroic code – lof/lofgeornost (most eager for praise/glory), showing this was seen as a virtue.
